Chapter 1

ONE

It was a bright cold day in April, and the clocks were striking thirteen. Winston Smith hurried through the glass doors of Victory Mansions, a gritty swirl of dust following him into the hallway that smelt of boiled cabbage and old rag mats. The lift was useless—the electric current cut off during daylight hours as part of the economy drive in preparation for Hate Week—and so Winston, thirty-nine years old with a varicose ulcer throbbing above his ankle, climbed seven flights past landing after landing where the enormous face of Big Brother gazed down from posters, the dark eyes following his every movement, the caption beneath declaring its ceaseless warning.

Inside his cramped flat, a telescreen babbled production figures from the wall—an instrument that could be dimmed but never silenced, that received and transmitted simultaneously, so that Winston existed always in the assumption that every sound he made was overheard, every gesture within range of that metal plaque scrutinized by the Thought Police. Through the window the world looked cold and colorless, the streets choked with dust and torn paper, the bombed-out ruins of nineteenth-century houses shored up with timber and cardboard. Only the posters provided color: Big Brother's black-mustachioed face plastered on every commanding corner, and in the distance the vast white pyramid of the Ministry of Truth soaring three hundred meters into the air, its three paradoxical slogans picked out in elegant lettering—WAR IS PEACE, FREEDOM IS SLAVERY, IGNORANCE IS STRENGTH.

Winston understood the architecture of oppression. Four colossal ministries divided the apparatus of government between them: Minitrue for propaganda, Minipax for war, Miniplenty for rationing scarcity, and Miniluv—windowless, fortified with barbed wire and machine-gun nests—for the maintenance of love through terror. He poured himself a teacup of Victory Gin, the colorless liquid burning like nitric acid, then retrieved from a drawer a peculiarly beautiful book with creamy paper and a marbled cover, purchased guiltily from a junk shop in violation of Party rules. What he was about to do was open a diary—not illegal, since nothing was illegal anymore, but punishable nonetheless by death or twenty-five years in a forced-labor camp.

In a shallow alcove hidden from the telescreen's sight, Winston dipped an archaic pen into ink and wrote the date: April 4th, 1984—though he could not be certain of the year, for nowadays it was impossible to pin down any date within a year or two. The words would not come at first, and then they poured out in panicked confession: a visit to the flicks, war films, refugees bombed in the Mediterranean, a child's arm spiraling into the air while the Party seats applauded. The memory triggered another—that morning's Two Minutes Hate, when Emmanuel Goldstein's face had flashed onto the telescreen and the entire Records Department had erupted into frenzy, screaming, kicking, frothing with manufactured rage that felt nonetheless horribly real. Winston had noticed two figures amid the crowd: a dark-haired girl from the Fiction Department whom he instinctively feared as a possible spy, and O'Brien, a burly Inner Party member whose fleeting glance seemed to communicate secret understanding, a momentary flash of solidarity that vanished as quickly as it appeared.

Afterward, sitting with the gin rising in his stomach, Winston discovered he had been writing unconsciously, the pen moving of its own volition across half a page: DOWN WITH BIG BROTHER, over and over in large neat capitals. It was absurd to panic—the act of opening the diary had already condemned him. Thoughtcrime could not be concealed forever. Sooner or later they were bound to get you, always at night, the rough hand shaking your shoulder, and then you were vaporized, your existence denied and forgotten.

He wrote once more in hurried scrawl—*theyll shoot me i dont care*—then laid down the pen, slightly ashamed of himself, when suddenly there came a knocking at the door.

Chapter 2

II

Winston stood frozen at his door, acutely aware that behind him the diary lay open on the table, its pages covered with the damning words DOWN WITH BIG BROTHER in letters nearly visible across the room. An inconceivably stupid thing to have done—yet even in his panic, he had not wanted to smudge the creamy paper by closing the book while the ink remained wet. He drew breath and opened the door.

Relief flooded through him. It was only Mrs. Parsons, a colorless, crushed-looking woman from the flat down the passage, her wispy hair framing a face so lined one had the impression dust had settled into its creases. She needed help with a blocked sink—her husband Tom was not home. Winston followed her through Victory Mansions, that crumbling relic from 1930 or thereabouts, where plaster flaked from walls, pipes burst in every frost, and repairs required sanction from remote committees that might delay even a window-pane mending for two years.

The Parsons flat presented a different species of dinginess: everything battered and trampled, as though visited by some large violent animal. Sports equipment littered the floor, dirty dishes crowded the table, and scarlet Youth League banners hung alongside a full-sized poster of Big Brother. The air carried the building's omnipresent boiled-cabbage smell, shot through with a sharper reek of sweat belonging to someone not present—Tom Parsons, that fattish man of paralyzing stupidity, a mass of imbecile enthusiasms upon whom the Party's stability depended more reliably than upon the Thought Police themselves.

Winston knelt to clear the pipe, extracting a clot of human hair from the filthy greenish water. He had barely returned to the living room when a savage voice yelled "Up with your hands!" The Parsons children—a tough-looking boy of nine and his younger sister, both in the blue shorts and red neckerchiefs of the Spies—menaced him with toy weapons, shrieking accusations of thought-crime and treason. There was calculating ferocity in the boy's eyes, a quite evident desire to strike. It was somehow slightly frightening, like the gamboling of tiger cubs that will soon grow into man-eaters.

Mrs. Parsons explained nervously that they were disappointed about missing the public hanging of Eurasian prisoners in the Park. As Winston took his leave, something struck the back of his neck with agonizing force—a catapult shot. The boy bellowed "Goldstein!" as Mrs. Parsons dragged him inside, her grayish face frozen in helpless fright.

Back in his flat, Winston reflected on the horror of it. That wretched woman lived in terror of her own children, and within a year or two they would be watching her night and day for symptoms of unorthodoxy. Through organizations like the Spies, children were systematically transformed into ungovernable savages whose ferocity was directed entirely outward—against foreigners, traitors, thought-criminals. Hardly a week passed without the Times celebrating some eavesdropping "child hero" who had denounced a parent to the Thought Police.

His thoughts drifted to O'Brien, to that dream from seven years ago: a pitch-dark room, a voice saying quietly, "We shall meet in the place where there is no darkness." He could not be certain whether O'Brien was friend or enemy, yet there existed between them a link of understanding more important than affection or partisanship.

The telescreen announced a glorious victory, then revealed that next week's chocolate ration would be reduced. Winston stood at the window watching the torn INGSOC poster flap in the wind, feeling utterly alone—the past dead, the future unimaginable, Big Brother's eyes pursuing him from coins and stamps and cigarette packets. Yet curiously, as the telescreen struck fourteen, something like resolve hardened within him. He returned to the diary and wrote his message to a future age when thought might be free, acknowledging that thoughtcrime did not merely entail death—thoughtcrime *was* death. He was already a dead man. Now it became important only to stay alive as long as possible, to scrub the telltale ink from his fingers, to place a single grain of dust upon the diary's cover so he might know if it had been disturbed.

The hour pressed forward, and Winston prepared to return to his work at the Ministry of Truth, carrying within him the weight of his impossible act and the fragile hope that somewhere, somehow, his words might outlast his annihilation.

Chapter 3

III

Winston dreamed of his mother.

She appeared to him as he remembered her from childhood—tall, statuesque, with magnificent fair hair and the slow movements of someone who had learned silence as a language. His father remained a vaguer figure: dark, thin, perpetually dressed in neat clothes with paper-thin soles on his shoes, spectacles perched on his face. Both had been swallowed up in the great purges of the Fifties, disappeared into that machinery of erasure that consumed so many and asked no forgiveness afterward.

In the dream, his mother sat far below him in some subterranean place—the bottom of a well, or a sinking ship's saloon—holding his infant sister in her arms. The two of them gazed upward through darkening green water, sinking ever downward while Winston remained in light and air. They were drowning *because* he lived. He knew this, and they knew it, and yet their faces held no reproach. Only acceptance of the unavoidable order of things.

What struck Winston upon reflection was not the imagery but the revelation it carried: his mother's death had possessed a quality of tragedy that no longer existed in the world. Tragedy required privacy, love, loyalty—concepts belonging to an ancient time when family members stood by one another without requiring ideological justification. She had died loving him, sacrificing herself to private principles he had been too young and selfish to reciprocate. Such things could not happen today. Today there existed fear, hatred, and pain, but no dignity of emotion. No deep or complex sorrows.

The dream shifted. He found himself standing on springy turf in golden evening light—the landscape he privately called the Golden Country. An old rabbit-bitten pasture with elm trees stirring like women's hair, a stream somewhere nearby where dace swam in willow-shaded pools. The dark-haired girl approached across the field and, in one fluid motion, tore off her clothes with magnificent carelessness. Her nakedness aroused nothing in him; what overwhelmed him was the gesture itself. That single splendid movement of the arm seemed to annihilate Big Brother, the Party, the Thought Police—seemed to belong to the ancient time when such defiance remained possible.

Winston woke with the word "Shakespeare" on his lips.

The telescreen shrieked him into consciousness at nought seven fifteen. He wrenched his naked body from bed—pajamas cost six hundred of his annual three thousand clothing coupons—and was immediately seized by a violent coughing fit that left his veins swollen and his varicose ulcer itching. The Physical Jerks commenced within minutes. A scrawny, muscular instructress barked commands through the telescreen while Winston mechanically shot his arms back and forth, wearing the expression of grim enjoyment considered proper for such exercises.

As he moved, his mind worked backward into childhood. The effort proved extraordinarily difficult. Beyond the late Fifties, everything faded into uncertainty. He remembered fragments: an air raid that surprised everyone, perhaps when the atomic bomb fell on Colchester. His father's hand clutching his own as they descended a spiral staircase into a Tube station. An old man reeking of gin, weeping over some unbearable grief, repeating that they shouldn't have trusted *them*—though Winston could no longer recall which buggers merited such distrust.

War had been continuous since that time, though not always the same war. The current arrangement placed Oceania against Eurasia, allied with Eastasia. Yet Winston knew—possessed the furtive, dangerous knowledge—that only four years ago the alliances had been reversed. The Party denied this. The Party had *always* denied it. The enemy of the moment represented absolute evil, and therefore had always been the enemy.

This was the terrifying heart of it: the Party's ability to thrust its hand into the past and declare *it never happened*. "Who controls the past controls the future: who controls the present controls the past." Reality control. Doublethink. The capacity to know and not know, to hold contradictory truths simultaneously, to forget and remember at will. Even understanding the word required practicing it.

The instructress called for toe-touches. Winston loathed this exercise, but when she singled him out by number—"6079 Smith W!"—he bent lower, his face betraying nothing, and for the first time in years managed to touch his toes with unbent knees, his mind already turning toward the unmistakable documentary proof of falsification he had once held in his hands.

Chapter 4

IV

Winston began his workday with an unconscious sigh—a breath of weariness that even the telescreen's omnipresent surveillance could not suppress—and drew the speakwrite toward him, blowing dust from its mouthpiece before settling into his cubicle in the Records Department. His workspace was equipped with the essential machinery of his trade: pneumatic tubes delivering messages and newspapers, and the memory hole, that ubiquitous slot through which every scrap of inconvenient documentation vanished into hidden furnaces below.

The morning's assignments arrived as four cryptic slips, each bearing instructions in the abbreviated jargon of the Ministry. Three were routine matters: Big Brother's speech had predicted a Eurasian offensive in North Africa when the attack had actually materialized in South India; production forecasts required adjustment to match output figures that were themselves fabrications; the Ministry of Plenty's categorical pledge regarding chocolate rations needed revision, since the promised stability would shortly become a reduction from thirty grams to twenty. Winston corrected each discrepancy with practiced efficiency, feeding his alterations into the pneumatic system while consigning the originals to the memory hole's consuming flames.

The machinery of falsification extended far beyond Winston's cubicle. Throughout the windowless hall, workers murmured into speakwrites, each engaged in their particular form of historical revision. A sandy-haired woman spent her days erasing the names of vaporized persons—her own husband among those who had ceased to exist. Ampleforth, a dreamy man with hairy ears, labored over ideologically offensive poems, producing sanitized versions for the anthologies. Beyond this hall lay printing shops, photography studios equipped for forgery, voice actors, reference clerks, and somewhere the anonymous directing brains who determined which fragments of the past should be preserved, which falsified, and which obliterated entirely.

Yet Winston understood that even the word "forgery" missed the essential absurdity of his work. The statistics he manipulated bore no connection to reality in either their original or rectified forms. Boot production figures were astronomical fantasies while half of Oceania went barefoot. Everything faded into a shadow-world where even the calendar year grew uncertain.

The fourth message presented a more intricate challenge, one that stirred in Winston something approaching professional satisfaction. Comrade Withers, once a prominent Inner Party member decorated for his work with the FFCC, had become an unperson. The organization had dissolved; Withers had simply ceased to exist. Winston's task was to rewrite Big Brother's Order for the Day so thoroughly that no trace of Withers remained. Across the hall, the secretive Tillotson was almost certainly working on the same assignment—perhaps a dozen workers were producing rival versions, from which some master brain would eventually select the chosen lie.

Rather than merely reverse the speech's content, Winston invented something entirely new: Comrade Ogilvy, a model Party member who had never drawn breath. He dictated the biography with fluency—the child who refused all toys except weapons, the boy who denounced his uncle to the Thought Police, the young man who designed a hand grenade that killed thirty-one Eurasian prisoners, the hero who weighted himself with his machine gun and leapt into the Indian Ocean rather than surrender his despatches. Ogilvy was a total abstainer, celibate, devoted to nothing but Ingsoc and the destruction of enemies.

When Winston finished, he felt a curious certainty that his version would prevail over Tillotson's. Comrade Ogilvy, unimagined an hour before, was now a fact. One could create dead men, it seemed, though not living ones—and once the act of forgery was forgotten, Ogilvy would exist upon precisely the same evidence as Charlemagne or Julius Caesar, his fabricated heroism indistinguishable from authentic history.

The morning's work complete, Winston sat amid the endless rustle of papers and the hum of voices reshaping the past, though even this practiced numbness could not entirely suppress the thoughts beginning to stir beneath the surface of his obedience.

Chapter 5

V

Deep beneath the Ministry of Truth, in a canteen thick with the sour reek of metallic stew and Victory Gin, Winston shuffled through the lunch queue alongside his colleague Syme—a philologist engaged in compiling the Eleventh Edition of the Newspeak dictionary. The word "friend" no longer existed in any meaningful sense; one had comrades, and Syme was among the more tolerable of them, though his mournful, protuberant eyes seemed always to be searching your face for something incriminating. When Syme asked about razor blades, Winston lied instinctively, hoarding his two unused blades while claiming he'd used the same one for six weeks. Such petty deceits had become reflexive. The Party shops failed perpetually—buttons one month, shoelaces the next, razor blades now—and survival demanded furtive scrounging on the so-called free market.

Over greasy trays of pinkish-gray stew and milkless Victory Coffee, Syme launched into rapturous discourse on his work. The beauty of Newspeak, he explained with pedantic passion, lay not in creating words but in destroying them—cutting language to the bone until thoughtcrime became literally impossible. Every synonym eliminated, every antonym collapsed into its opposite: "ungood" instead of "bad," "plusgood" instead of "excellent." By 2050, he predicted, no human being would remain capable of understanding their present conversation. The whole literature of the past—Chaucer, Shakespeare, Milton—would exist only in Newspeak versions, transformed into something contradictory of what they once were. "Orthodoxy is unconsciousness," Syme declared with mystical satisfaction.

Winston listened and smiled when required, but a cold certainty settled over him: Syme would be vaporized. He was too intelligent, saw too clearly, spoke too plainly. The Party did not tolerate such dangerous clarity, however orthodox the vessel containing it.

Their meal was interrupted by Parsons, Winston's neighbor from Victory Mansions—a sweating, boyish man who seemed perpetually dressed in the invisible uniform of the Spies he had never outgrown. He collected Winston's subscription for Hate Week and boasted proudly of his daughter's vigilance: the seven-year-old had followed a stranger for two hours and handed him over to the patrols, suspicious of his foreign-looking shoes. The man's fate remained unspoken but unmistakable—Parsons mimed a rifle shot and clicked his tongue.

A telescreen announcement blared triumphantly about increased production and a raised chocolate ration—twenty grams weekly, presented as bounty. Winston alone seemed to remember that yesterday the ration had been announced as a reduction to twenty grams. Parsons swallowed it with animal stupidity; the quacking orthodox man at the next table swallowed it fanatically; even Syme swallowed it through some complex mechanism of doublethink. Was Winston alone in possessing memory?

He surveyed the grimy canteen with its battered furniture and greasy surfaces, the ugly, ill-favored Party members who bore no resemblance to the blond, muscular ideal of the propaganda posters. Always there was that protest in the stomach, that sense of being cheated of something one had a right to possess.

Then a violent jerk pulled him from his reverie. The dark-haired girl from the Fiction Department had turned and was watching him with curious intensity. Terror flooded Winston's spine—was she Thought Police, or worse, an amateur spy? He could not recall if his features had been properly controlled during his treasonous reflections. When the telescreen whistle summoned them back to work, the three men sprang up, and the tobacco fell from Winston's carefully hoarded cigarette—one more small loss in a life built entirely of them.

The girl's watching eyes lingered in his mind like a splinter, and Winston carried that unease with him as he rejoined the labyrinthine corridors of the Ministry, uncertain whether he was being hunted or merely haunted by his own paranoia.

Chapter 6

VI

Winston sat hunched over his diary, forcing himself to record a memory that clawed at him with particular viciousness. Three years prior, on a dim evening near one of the railway stations, he had encountered a woman standing beneath a barely functional street lamp. Her face had been painted thick and white, lips bright red—a mask that drew him precisely because Party women never painted their faces. The absence of telescreens, the offered price of two dollars, the narrow street empty of witnesses: he had followed her through a doorway, across a backyard, down into a basement kitchen.

But the writing came hard. Winston found himself pressing his fingers against his closed eyes, fighting the urge to scream obscenities, to smash his head against the wall, to hurl the inkpot through the window. One's worst enemy, he reflected with the clinical detachment that had become habit, was one's own nervous system. He recalled passing a man in the street weeks before—an ordinary-looking Party member whose face had twitched involuntarily, a spasm rapid as a camera shutter. That poor devil was done for. The body betrayed what the mind struggled to conceal, and the deadliest danger of all was talking in one's sleep. Against that, no guard existed.

The memory of the basement kitchen conjured another figure: Katharine, his wife. Married still, technically, though they had separated nearly eleven years ago after just fifteen months together. The Party forbade divorce but encouraged separation when no children resulted. Katharine had possessed a noble, aquiline face that promised depth yet delivered nothing—a mind composed entirely of slogans, a human sound-track for Party orthodoxy. Winston might have endured her vapidity had it not been for the matter of sex. To embrace her was to embrace a wooden image; she would lie with eyes shut, neither resisting nor cooperating, merely submitting. Yet she had insisted they continue the weekly performance—"making a baby," she called it, or "our duty to the Party"—until finally, mercifully, no child appeared and they parted.

This was, Winston understood, precisely what the Party intended. Its undeclared purpose was not merely to prevent loyalties beyond its control but to drain all pleasure from the sexual act, to transform eroticism into something disgusting and mechanical. Organizations like the Junior Anti-Sex League advocated complete celibacy; Newspeak had already coined *artsem* for artificial insemination. Through careful conditioning—cold water, martial music, endless slogans—the Party had rendered its women impregnable walls of chastity. And so Winston was left with furtive encounters in prole districts, joyless transactions tacitly tolerated because they posed no threat to Party power.

He forced himself to continue writing. In the lamplight of that basement kitchen, the woman had been revealed at last: old, fifty at least, her thick paint cracking like a cardboard mask, her mouth fallen open to expose only cavernous blackness where teeth should have been. He had gone ahead and done it just the same.

Winston pressed his fingers to his eyes once more. The confession lay on the page, yet the therapy had not worked. The urge to scream remained as fierce as ever, and somewhere beneath it stirred a deeper longing—not merely for physical release, but for an act of genuine rebellion, a connection the Party could not control or defile.

It was a longing that would prove difficult to suppress.

Chapter 7

VII

*If there is hope, it lies in the proles.*

Winston inscribes this proposition in his diary with the desperate conviction of a man grasping at arithmetic in a world where numbers have become negotiable. The logic appears sound enough: the proles constitute eighty-five per cent of Oceania's population, a vast reservoir of potential force that could sweep the Party away like a horse shaking off flies. The Party cannot be overthrown from within—its enemies, if such creatures exist, cannot so much as identify one another. Rebellion among Party members amounts to nothing more than a certain look in the eyes, perhaps a whispered syllable. But the proles, requiring no conspiracy, needing only to become conscious of their own strength, could blow the entire apparatus to pieces tomorrow morning.

And yet.

Winston recalls walking through a crowded street when a tremendous cry erupted from hundreds of women's throats—a great formidable sound of anger and despair that set his heart leaping. A riot at last, the proles breaking loose. But when he reached the source, he found only a mob of two or three hundred women crowding round a market stall, their faces tragic over the matter of tin saucepans. The supply had given out. Two bloated women tore at the same pot until the handle came off. Winston watched with disgust, though he could not forget the frightening power that had sounded in that cry. Why could they never shout like that about anything that mattered?

He writes: *Until they become conscious they will never rebel, and until after they have rebelled they cannot become conscious.*

The Party, naturally, maintains contradictory positions on the proles. They were liberated from capitalist bondage, yet they remain natural inferiors requiring subjection. In practice, very little is known about them, and less is required. Left to themselves like cattle, they follow ancestral patterns—birth, gutters, work at twelve, marriage at twenty, middle age at thirty, death at sixty. Heavy labor, petty quarrels, football, beer, gambling. A few Thought Police agents move among them, eliminating the occasionally dangerous individual, but no attempt is made at ideological indoctrination. As the Party slogan states: *Proles and animals are free.*

Winston scratches his varicose ulcer and contemplates the impossibility of knowing what life before the Revolution was actually like. He copies passages from a children's history textbook describing the horrors of capitalism—starving children, cruel masters, fat ugly men in top hats. The *jus primae noctis*. Yet how much of it is lies? The only evidence to the contrary is the mute protest in one's own bones. Modern life's truly characteristic quality is not its cruelty but its bareness, its dinginess—slogging through dreary jobs, fighting for space on the Tube, darning worn socks.

Once, Winston possessed concrete evidence of falsification. A photograph from the *Times* showing Jones, Aaronson, and Rutherford at a Party function in New York on a date when, according to their confessions, they had been on Eurasian soil betraying military secrets. The confessions were lies. He had held proof between his fingers for thirty seconds before dropping it into the memory hole. The past not only changed, but changed continuously.

*I understand HOW*, he writes. *I do not understand WHY.*

He wonders if perhaps a lunatic is simply a minority of one. The Party would announce that two and two made five, and you would have to believe it. The heresy of heresies was common sense. Yet his courage stiffens unexpectedly as O'Brien's face floats into his mind. He is writing for O'Brien, to O'Brien. The obvious and the true must be defended. Truisms are true. Stones are hard, water is wet.

*Freedom is the freedom to say that two plus two make four. If that is granted, all else follows.*

With this axiom committed to paper, Winston has armed himself with the most dangerous weapon imaginable—simple arithmetic—though whether it will prove sufficient against an enemy capable of rewriting mathematics itself remains terrifyingly uncertain.

Chapter 8

VIII

The scent of real coffee drifting from a doorway arrested Winston mid-stride, transporting him for two fleeting seconds to the half-forgotten landscape of childhood before a slamming door severed the memory as cleanly as a blade. His varicose ulcer throbbed from kilometers of walking—this was his second missed evening at the Community Center in three weeks, a reckless indulgence in what Newspeak termed *ownlife*. Yet the April balminess had made the prospect of creaking camaraderie and gin-soaked lectures intolerable, and so he had wandered into the brown-colored prole slums north of what had once been Saint Pancras Station.

"If there is hope, it lies in the proles." The words from his diary repeated themselves like an incantation both mystical and absurd. Around him the streets swarmed with humanity—girls with crudely lipsticked mouths, waddling women who foretold their futures, barefooted children scattering through puddles. Two women fell silent at the sight of his blue overalls, regarding him with the wariness reserved for unfamiliar animals. A rocket bomb—"steamer," the proles called it—sent the street into commotion; Winston flung himself down as the pavement heaved. Rising, he found himself covered in glass fragments. Two hundred meters ahead, houses lay demolished beneath a black plume of smoke. A severed human hand, whitened with plaster dust except for its bloody stump, lay on the cobblestones. Winston kicked it into the gutter and walked on. Within minutes, the sordid life of the streets continued as though nothing had occurred.

Outside a pub, three proles argued with violent passion over lottery numbers—the Lottery being their principal reason for remaining alive, their anodyne and intellectual stimulant. Winston knew, as all Party members knew, that the prizes were largely imaginary, the winners nonexistent persons. Yet these people could perform staggering feats of memory over meaningless numbers while remaining incapable of comparing one age with another.

He found himself, as if guided by some suicidal instinct, outside the junk shop where he had purchased his diary. An old prole with prawn-like white mustaches disappeared through a pub doorway, and Winston followed—driven by the lunatic notion that this man, who must have been middle-aged before the Revolution, might provide truthful testimony about the capitalist past. Inside, he bought the old man drinks and pressed him for answers about oppression, about whether life had been better or worse before. But the old man's memory yielded only rubbish—quarrels, top hats, a drunken shove on Boat Race night. The relevant facts lay perpetually outside the range of his vision.

Winston entered the junk shop and encountered Mr. Charrington, a benevolent, spectacled proprietor who sold him a beautiful glass paperweight containing coral from the Indian Ocean—an object possessing the air of belonging to an utterly different age. Upstairs, a room without telescreen awakened in Winston an ancestral memory of security, of sitting utterly alone with nobody watching. Mr. Charrington taught him fragments of a nursery rhyme about London churches: *Oranges and lemons, say the bells of St. Clement's*.

Stepping onto the pavement humming the half-remembered tune, Winston's heart turned to ice: the dark-haired girl from the Fiction Department was walking toward him, not ten meters away. She looked him straight in the face, then passed on as though she had not seen him. The coincidence was impossible—she must be spying on him. He stumbled home, the glass paperweight banging against his thigh, his bowels liquid with terror, contemplating the torture that would inevitably precede his death.

Sitting before his diary that night, the Party slogans tolling through his mind like a leaden knell, Winston understood that the body always betrays in moments of crisis, that even now his physical anguish made consecutive thought impossible—and that somewhere ahead, in the place where there is no darkness, his fate already waited for him.

Chapter 9

TWO

The encounter happened in the middle of a morning, in that long, brightly lit corridor where nothing ought to occur except the mechanical transit of bodies between one place of work and another. Winston saw the dark-haired girl approaching—four days since he had spotted her outside the junk shop, four days of accumulating dread—and noted the sling on her arm, the same drab color as her overalls, likely the result of some routine crushing in the Fiction Department's machinery. Then she stumbled, fell hard on her injured side, and cried out in pain.

What happened next was a thing Winston could not have predicted in himself. Here was the enemy, the creature he had imagined smashing with a cobblestone, and yet he moved instinctively to help her, felt the echo of her pain in his own body. She rose pale and trembling, thanked him with the perfunctory word "comrade," and walked on. The whole business occupied perhaps half a minute, conducted in plain view of a telescreen, their faces betraying nothing. But in the seconds of contact, she had pressed something into his hand—a scrap of paper folded small.

He carried it to his cubicle, forced himself to wait five agonizing minutes, his heart hammering with a violence that seemed surely audible. The possibilities cycled through his mind: a trap laid by the Thought Police, a summons to death, or—and this wild hope refused to be suppressed—some communication from the Brotherhood, proof that resistance existed. When at last he unfolded the paper, three words met his eyes: *I love you.*

For the rest of that morning and the days that followed, Winston existed in a state of unbearable suspension. He concealed his agitation from the telescreens, endured Parsons's vapid enthusiasm about Hate Week preparations, performed his work of falsifying records, attended his obligatory evening at the Community Center—all while burning with the need to make contact. The difficulty was immense, every avenue of communication surveilled or foreclosed. Letters were opened as routine. Following her home meant loitering outside the Ministry. He did not even know her name.

After a week of near misses in the canteen—she on different shifts, surrounded by others, positioned beneath telescreens—Winston finally managed to sit at her table. They spoke in murmurs, faces down, spooning watery stew, exchanging the essential coordinates: Victory Square, nineteen hours, among a crowd where telescreens could not isolate their conversation.

He arrived early and waited beneath the fluted column topped by Big Brother's statue, dread tightening around him until he spotted her pretending to read a poster. The opportunity came when a convoy of Eurasian prisoners rolled through and the crowd surged forward. Winston was gelatinous with fatigue—the word arriving unbidden, perfectly apt. The crisis that had demanded such frantic labor was simple enough to describe, impossible to absorb. On the sixth day of Hate Week, amid the drumrolls and massed planes and booming guns, while the collective hatred of Eurasia was reaching its quivering climax and the crowd would gladly have torn apart the two thousand Eurasian war criminals scheduled for public hanging—at precisely that moment it was announced that Oceania was not at war with Eurasia. Oceania was at war with Eastasia. Eurasia was an ally. There was, of course, no admission that anything had changed. Winston had witnessed it himself: the Inner Party orator on the scarlet-draped platform, a grotesque Rumpelstiltskin figure gripping the microphone, had received a slip of paper mid-sentence and switched enemies without breaking syntax, without altering his tone, the names simply becoming different while the hatred continued uninterrupted. The Ministry's staff slept on mattresses in corridors, subsisted on sandwiches and Victory Coffee, and each time Winston returned from his three-hour rest periods he found fresh cylinders piled on his desk like snowdrifts. The work demanded care and imagination, geographical knowledge, the creative effort of making forgery perfect—and Winston found himself as anxious as anyone that it should be flawless, despite knowing that every word he murmured into the speakwrite was deliberate falsehood.
