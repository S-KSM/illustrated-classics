Chapter 1

The Feather

From Attar's "حکایت سیمرغ" — The Tale of the Simorgh.

"A feather fell at midnight, and the world has been arguing about beauty ever since."

There is a story they tell about how it began.

One night — a midnight so quiet that even the rivers held still — a great bird passed over China. Nobody saw it. Nobody could have. The Simorgh moves the way a thought moves: present, then gone, leaving only its weather behind.

A single feather slipped from her wing. Down it drifted, over walled cities and sleeping caravans, until it came to rest on a street where a painter had left his window open.

By morning, the whole country had heard.

A weaver said to her apprentice, "Bring me red. The deepest red you have."

A poet, who had given up writing years before, scratched a single line on the margin of a forgotten letter.

A child showed her father a drawing she could not explain. "Where did this come from?" he asked. She shrugged. "From inside."

Every imitation of beauty in this world — every song, every tile, every dome — is, at root, a copy of that one feather. A scholar later wrote: "Even if you must travel to China to find it, seek the knowledge." That phrase was about this feather. The feather is still there, in the great gallery, waiting.

The teachers say: if the feather had never fallen, the world would have been quieter. There would be nothing to chase. We would not have the cathedrals. We would not have the elegies. We would not even have these long arguments about what God means. The feather is the reason for our restlessness.

Now — the birds heard.

Of course they heard. They live in the air, and rumors of the Simorgh travel through the air the way scent travels through cloth.

They gathered, every species. Sparrows, falcons, ducks, peacocks, owls, nightingales — a parliament with feathers. The clearing was loud. Then the Hoopoe — the bird who had once carried letters for King Solomon, who knew the ways of the road — flew to the highest stone and raised her crowned head.

"Listen," she said. "You feel something. I know. You have felt it your whole lives. The ache. The not-quite. The sense that you are missing someone you have never met. That feeling has a source, and the source has a name. Her name is the Simorgh, and she is our true sovereign. We are her subjects, and we have spent our lives in exile from her court."

A hush.

"There is a road. It is real. It is long. Most of you will not finish. But the ones who do — and even the ones who only begin — will discover that the road itself is the answer to the ache. Will you come?"

They roared yes.

Hearts pounded. Wings flexed. The dream of a true king — a king who could not die, could not be deposed, could not be misunderstood — set the whole clearing on fire.

"Then begin," said the Hoopoe.

But here, of course, the trouble started.

The road was long. Very long. The kind of long that tests the difference between wanting and committing. As the birds began to think about what the journey would cost, each of them found a reason to hesitate.

The peacock fluffed his tail and considered the dust.

The parrot inspected her gold collar.

The duck looked at her clean little pond.

The falcon glanced toward the king's window, where his hood and glove were waiting.

One by one, they began to clear their throats. They had things to say.

Excuses, in other words. The longest list of excuses ever given in the literature of the world.

The Hoopoe looked at all of them — at every familiar evasion she had been expecting — and sat down.

"Speak," she said gently. "I will answer each of you in turn."

This is where our book really begins. Every chapter that follows is one bird's excuse, and one of the Hoopoe's answers. Read closely. Many of these excuses will sound like yours. Many of the answers, if you sit with them, will be the answer you have been waiting for too.

The feather has fallen on you also. Why else would you be reading this?

— end of chapter 1 —

Chapter 2

The Nightingale

The first excuse: aestheticism. A love that mistakes the gift for the giver.

The Nightingale flew to the front and perched on the highest branch.

He was a small brown bird with the most beautiful voice in the world, and he carried that voice the way a poet carries a manuscript he expects to be famous.

"Friends," he sang. "I am the singer of love. Every flute crying in the night is borrowing my grief. Every guitar weeping in the dark is borrowing my chord. I am the source of every love song that has ever existed."

He paused for the appreciation he was used to.

"And the secret of my art is that I am, myself, in love."

A murmur. He extended one wing toward a great red rose blooming nearby.

"With Her. The Rose. My beloved. My beautiful. Every morning she opens her petals — for me. Every dusk she folds them — missing me. I cannot leave on this absurd quest. My Rose needs me. Without my songs she would wilt; without her face I would die. The Simorgh is too far, and too abstract. My Rose is here, and she is enough."

He bowed, expecting agreement.

The Hoopoe alighted in front of him and was quiet for a long moment before she spoke.

"Little brother. Your Rose is beautiful. I will not say otherwise. But have you noticed the calendar?"

"What about it?"

"In a week, her petals brown and fall. By winter she is ash and wood. Every spring she returns — but she is a different Rose, with no memory of you. You sing to her each year as though she remembers your last song. She does not. The Rose does not belong to you. She belongs to the wind."

The Nightingale opened his mouth and shut it again.

"You love how she makes you feel," the Hoopoe continued. "That is not the same as loving her. And she is, herself, also a gift — a beautiful gift — but she is not the Giver. To stay home for the gift while the Giver is calling you to the road: that is the oldest mistake in our species. Let me tell you a story."

Once there was a king with a daughter so beautiful that the city moved around her like water around a stone. Her cheeks were pale snow. Her hair smelled of orchards. Her smile, by all reports, could undo a man's name.

In that same city lived a beggar — bone-thin, barefoot, holding a small piece of bread he had bought for his dinner. He was crossing the street when the princess passed in her carriage.

He looked up.

The bread fell from his hand and rolled into the dust. He did not notice.

The princess, looking down, saw him: a ridiculous, openmouthed creature with bread crumbs on his chin. She laughed. A small, polite, slightly amused laugh. The carriage rolled on.

The beggar dropped to his knees in the street.

"She smiled at me," he whispered.

He did not go home. He sat at the corner of her palace and waited for another smile. Days passed. Weeks. Months. Years. The street dogs came and curled around him, and he did not notice them either. He grew thin and wild-haired, and the palace servants began to whisper about the strange beggar at the gate.

After seven years they decided to kill him. The princess overheard. That night she came down to the gate herself, hooded, and shook him gently awake.

"They mean to murder you. Run."

He looked up at her. His eyes were full of stars and dirt.

"My princess, I have been already dead since the day I saw you smile. They cannot kill what is already gone. But before I die — please — answer one question."

"What."

"That smile. Seven years ago. What did it mean?"

She looked at him for a long moment, and what she said next was the most terrible thing the beggar had ever heard, because it was true.

"You poor fool. I laughed because you looked ridiculous. The bread had fallen in the dust. Your mouth was hanging open. I felt sorry for you, in the way one feels sorry for a stray dog. To laugh at you is permitted. To imagine I laughed for you was your error. That was your error."

She turned and walked away, and — as Attar puts it in the original — whatever there had been was, in the end, nothing at all.

The Hoopoe let the silence settle.

"Little Nightingale. The Rose smiles. The Rose does not smile for you. The Rose smiles whether you are there or not. To love what is indifferent to you, and call it your beloved, is to spend your life on a doorstep that was never yours."

"Then what should I love?"

"The Gardener. The One who made the Rose, and the wind, and your voice, and the seasons. Loving the Source means you will never be ridiculous, and you will never be left at a gate. Loving the Source means every flower you encounter is a real letter from a real Friend — instead of a stranger's polite laugh."

The Nightingale stared at his Rose. He saw, perhaps for the first time, that she was just opening and closing on her own clock, indifferent to him.

"Will you come?"

He hesitated. "Let me think."

"Take your time," said the Hoopoe. "We are not leaving without you, if we can help it."

Read this chapter twice if you have ever loved someone who did not see you. The Nightingale is not a fool; he is most of us. The point is not that beauty is bad. The point is that beauty is a letter, and we have been kissing the envelope.

— end of chapter 2 —
